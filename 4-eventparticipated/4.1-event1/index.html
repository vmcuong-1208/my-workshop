1. Addicted to Learning Like You’re Addicted to Social Media

This talk is part of a series of technology-sharing events organized by Amazon Web Services (AWS), focusing on the topic of Context Engineering — the art of providing the right context to maximize the power of AI models.

Speakers

  • Huynh Hoang Long – Admin of FCAJ.

Summary of content and main activities

The talk was structured in five activities that move from neuroscience toward practical action: (1) why our brain chooses social media over studying, (2) how to turn knowledge into a dopamine gamble, (3) three brain hacks to make learning feel like playing, (4) stealing the formula used by social media - assisted by AI, and (5) student-friendly AI projects to start with right now.

  • Activity 1 — Why Does Your Brain Choose Social Media Over Studying

    • Why does the brain prioritize social media/gaming over learning? (Quick rewards, curiosity, low motivation)?
    • There is a contrast between studying and leisure activities such as playing games or browsing social media. The brain often prioritizes things that provide quick rewards (instant results) and are highly intriguing, while studying usually yields slow results, causing the brain to quickly become bored.

  • Activity 2 — Turn Knowledge Into a Dopamine Gamble

    • Dopamine Mechanism: How Habit Streaks and Rewards Create Brain Stimulation.
    • Dopamine is released when we feel satisfied or anticipate a reward. To develop a learning addiction, you need to create a process: Curiosity -> Challenge/Explore -> Receive Reward -> Satisfaction -> Repeat. Writing small rewards on a piece of paper and drawing lots is a practical example of tricking the brain into releasing dopamine regularly.

  • Activity 3 — Three Brains Hacks to Make Learning Feel Like Playing

    • Practical tips:

      • Use the fear of “losing a streak” to maintain habits: People tend to fear loss more than they want to gain. Maintaining a continuous learning streak (similar to attendance checks in games or social media streaks) creates positive pressure, making you fear missing the streak you’ve worked so hard to build.

      • The rule of breaking down goals (breaking down a huge amount of knowledge): Instead of being intimidated by a huge amount of knowledge, break it down into tiny tasks (e.g., study for 5-10 minutes, create an account, solve a simple math problem). This helps the brain easily start without feeling pressured.

      • The 2-minute rule (quickly solve simple tasks): Anything that can be solved within 2 minutes should be done immediately to avoid procrastination.

      • Create a conducive learning environment (invest in a dedicated study area) and build a feedback system: Establish a feedback system such as awarding experience points (XP) after each study session or rewarding yourself with a cup of coffee upon achieving a goal. Leveling up or receiving rewards will convince the brain that studying is as satisfying as playing games.

      • Build a positive learning environment and habits: Decorating your study desk beautifully and maintaining a consistent study space plays a crucial role in signaling the brain to automatically “get to work” whenever you sit down.

  • Activity 4 — Steal the Fomula Used by Social Media

    • Learn how AI usage is evolving: from a single question → grounded with documentation → personalized over time. Explore the concept of a “Second AI Brain” and the Store → Retrieve → Generate → Learn process.

  • Activity 5 — Suggesting student-friendly AI projects

    • The speaker suggested four projects for students to start: AI Study Assistant (summarizing notes, creating quizzes), PDF Chat App (Q&A based on study materials), AI Code Reviewer (explaining bugs, suggesting improvements), and Personal Second Brain (storing and retrieving notes).

Results and values ​​achieved

  • Lessons learned

    • The brain does not lack willpower - it lacks a fast, consistent reward loop around studying. Designing curiosity -> challenge -> reward -> repeat is more effective than forcing discipline.

    • Loss aversion is a stronger motivator than gain - a daily streak is much more sticky than a daily reward.

    • Tiny tasks beat big goals on day one. Starting with a 5-10 minute task is far easier for the brain than planning a 4-hour study sprint.

    • External cues matter: a beautiful, dedicated study space turns the act of sitting down into a psychological ‘start working’ trigger.

    • AI tools can become part of the reward loop (Second AI Brain, RAG-based study assistant), not just a productivity tool - they can make studying feel as interactive as browsing social media.

  • Newly Acquired Skills

    • Self-Awareness & Behavioral Psychology: Understanding the dopamine mechanism, loss aversion, the 2-minute rule and chunking reasons behind everyday procrastination.

    • Habit Design: Ability to engineer a personal habit loop: small task -> commitment streak -> immediate reward -> satisfying feedback -> repeat.

    • Learning System Design: Familiarity with the Second AI Brain concept (Store -> Retrieve -> Generate -> Learn) and how AI can personalize long-term studying.

    • Practical AI Awareness: Knowledge of four student-friendly AI projects to start with (Study Assistant, PDF Chat, Code Reviewer, Personal Second Brain).

    • Soft Skills: Practice in reflecting honestly on my own learning habits, formulating honest questions to the speaker, and sharing concrete routines with classmates after the session.

Closing Note

Huynh Hoang Long’s ‘Addicted to Learning’ session reframed studying not as a test of discipline but as a product to design. By giving the brain the right dopamine loops, breaking goals down, designing a friendly environment and pairing everything with an AI ‘Second Brain’, learning can feel as natural as scrolling a feed. As a student I walked away with a concrete personal action plan, a better toolkit to support my teammates, and an honest reminder that learning - like any habit - is built one small, intentional loop at a time.

2. Automated Prompt Engineering - Enhancing LLM Output Quality

A technical AI talk hosted inside the First Cloud AI Journey community track, framed as ‘The Art of Communicating with AI.’ The session walked through why prompt engineering is a first-class engineering discipline (not magic words), the core components of a well-designed prompt, advanced prompting techniques (CoT, ToT, Self-Consistency, RAG, Role Prompting), token economics of frontier models (Claude Opus 4.6 pricing), and the speaker’s own AWS-native productionised solution - Proptimizer, a browser extension that optimises prompts inline. The architecture behind Proptimizer (Bedrock, Lambda, API Gateway, Cognito, S3, CloudFront, DynamoDB, CloudWatch) was dissected slide by slide and served as a concrete capstone-grade example of how to ship a small LLM product end-to-end on AWS.

Speakers

  • Nguyen Tuan Thinh – DevOps Engineer, First Cloud AI Journey.

Summary of content and main activities

Nguyen Tuan Thinh framed the talk as ‘The Art of Communicating with AI’ but quickly made clear it is a craft. He opened with the cost of bad prompts: generic prompts yield generic outputs, ambiguous instructions produce inconsistent results, verbose prompts burn tokens and budget, and poor communication eats productivity. He then laid out the anatomy of a great prompt (Role, Instruction, Context, Input Data, Output Format, Examples, Constraints), the seven principles of effective prompting, and a tour of advanced techniques (CoT, ToT, Self-Consistency, RAG, Role Prompting) with concrete before/after examples. Token economics were addressed with real numbers (Claude Opus 4.6 at $5 / 1M input tokens, $25 / 1M output tokens), leading to the speaker’s product - Proptimizer, a browser extension that optimises prompts in-place. The session closed with a slide-by-slide walkthrough of Proptimizer’s AWS architecture (Bedrock + Lambda + API Gateway + Cognito + S3 + CloudFront + DynamoDB + CloudWatch) and a working URL for the audience to try the tool themselves.

  • Activity 1 — Opening: The Art of Communicating with AI

    • Set the tone with a ‘communication is craft’ framing. No hype, no magic words promise. Stressed that prompting is a learnable engineering discipline, not a personality trait of the LLM.

  • Activity 2 — Problem Statement - Why Prompt Engineering Matters

    • Articulated the four costs of bad prompts in one slide. (1) Generic prompts → generic outputs. (2) Wasted tokens → increased costs. (3) Ambiguous instructions → inconsistent results. (4) Poor communication → lost productivity. The sum is the business case for the rest of the talk.

  • Activity 3 — Anatomy of a Great Prompt (The 7 Components)

    • Broke down a well-designed prompt into seven components. (1) Role - who the model is playing (a lawyer, a junior dev, a reviewer). (2) Instruction - what to do. (3) Context - background the model should know. (4) Input Data - the user’s actual content. (5) Output Format - structured shape, not free text. (6) Examples (few-shot) - 1-3 worked examples. (7) Constraints/Guidelines - hard rules the answer must respect.

  • Activity 4 — Principle 1 - Be Clear and Specific

    • First of seven principles of effective prompting. Vague instructions produce vague answers; the model has no signal of what ‘good’ looks like. Specificity is the cheapest win.

  • Activity 5 — Principle 2 - Use Directive Language

    • Tell the model what to produce, not what you would prefer not to see. ‘Write three sentences’ outperforms ’try not to write too much’.

  • Activity 6 - Principle 3 - Use Delimiters to Separate Sections

    • Use clear tags (triple backticks, XML tags, JSON keys) to separate Role, Context, Input and Output Format sections so the model does not mix them up. Cheap, immediate quality win.

  • Activity 7 - Principle 4 - Describe DOs, Not DON’Ts

    • Positive instructions outperform negative ones for most frontier models. ‘Only respond with JSON’ beats ‘don’t write prose’. This reframes a habit most students bring from earlier rule-based systems.

  • Activity 8 - Principle 5 - Don’t Ask LLMs to Do Math

    • Frontier models are language models, not calculators. If the task involves precision maths or counting, run a deterministic computation and pass the result back to the LLM as input. Avoids token-flavoured arithmetic errors.

  • Activity 9 - Principle 6 - Allow ‘I Don’t Know’ Responses

    • Reduce hallucinations by explicitly letting the model say ‘I don’t know’ or ‘I need more context’. Increases answer reliability and trust, especially for domain-specific assistants.

  • Activity 10 - Principle 7 - Break Long Inputs into Smaller Segments

    • Long-context fatigue is real - models get lazier as the prompt grows. Split long documents into smaller chunks with clear instructions per chunk, then aggregate. Especially relevant for student projects that try to dump a whole book into a prompt.

  • Activity 11 - Advanced Technique 1 - Chain-of-Thought (CoT) Prompting

    • Ask the model to think step-by-step. Improves accuracy on multi-step reasoning and produces a trace you can audit. The canonical ’let’s think step by step’ family of patterns.

  • Activity 12 - Advanced Technique 2 - Self-Consistency

    • Run the same CoT prompt multiple times with temperature > 0 and pick the most common answer. Trades compute for accuracy; useful for classification and reasoning benchmarks.

  • Activity 13 - Advanced Technique 3 - Tree-of-Thoughts (ToT)

    • Generalises CoT into a search tree: branch ideas, evaluate partial solutions, prune. Strong for complex planning, debugging and design-space reasoning.

  • Activity 14 - Advanced Technique 4 - Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)

    • Inject external knowledge via a retriever so the model answers from fresh, grounded context rather than from possibly stale training data. Foundational pattern for production chatbots and student capstone assistants.

  • Activity 15 - Advanced Technique 5 - Role Prompting

    • Assigning the model a persona (e.g. ‘You are a careful senior code reviewer’) consistently shapes tone, vocabulary and depth. Combined with few-shot examples, gives very stable outputs across calls.

  • Activity 16 - Token Economics - Anthropic Claude Opus 4.6 Pricing

    • Made the cost concrete. Claude Opus 4.6 charges $5.00 per 1M input tokens and $25.00 per 1M output tokens. Hooked the talk into production economics: every verbose prompt is billable, and prompt optimisation is also cost optimisation.

  • Activity 17 - AWS Bedrock - Foundation Model Gateway

    • Introduced Amazon Bedrock as the easy way to call frontier models - Claude, GPT and others - from a single AWS-native API without managing the underlying model infrastructure. Positioned it as the default LLM backbone for any AWS-hosted AI product.

  • Activity 18 - Proptimizer - The Speaker’s Own Solution

    • Introduced Proptimizer: a browser extension that optimises prompts and lets users chat with AI anywhere on the web. The product is positioned as ‘paste → optimise → send’, eliminating the gap between thinking about a prompt and shipping it.

  • Activity 19 - Proptimizer Architecture - Layer 1 (Edge & Identity)

    • Layer 1 of the architecture. Amazon CloudFront serves the static frontend globally; Amazon S3 hosts the extension assets; Amazon Cognito provides user authentication and JWT management. Edge-first, fast even for first-time users.

  • Activity 20 - Proptimizer Architecture - Layer 2 (API & Compute)

    • Layer 2 of the architecture. Amazon API Gateway routes and rate-limits traffic to the backend; AWS Lambda runs the optimisation logic serverlessly; Amazon Bedrock is called from Lambda to do the actual prompt rewriting. No EC2 instance is ever managed by the team.

  • Activity 21 - Proptimizer Architecture - Layer 3 (State & Observability)

    • Layer 3 of the architecture. Amazon DynamoDB stores prompt history for each user (recent optimisations, favourite templates). Amazon CloudWatch monitors logs, latency and error rates. Closing the loop on cost, performance and user experience.

  • Activity 22 - Before / After Example - Generic vs Specific Summarisation

    • Side-by-side example showed the value of prompt quality. Generic prompt: ‘Summarize the following text.’ Refactored prompt: ‘You are a teacher. Summarize the following article for a high-school student in under 50 words, focusing on the main ideas.’ Same model, very different output, and very different cost.

  • Activity 23 - Before / After Example - Social Media Constraints

    • Second worked example. A vague ‘Write a social media post’ was rewritten into a tightly constrained brief: ‘Write an Instagram caption under 150 characters, target audience 18-30, with a clear call-to-action and 3-5 relevant hashtags.’ Showed how constraints + output format + role produce repeatable, on-brand output.

  • Activity 24 - Live Demo and Access (Proptimizer URL)

    • Closed with a working demo and the URL d3jqm7so635aqb.cloudfront.net, encouraging the audience to actually try Proptimizer instead of just nodding along. Also shared the speaker’s LinkedIn for follow-ups and questions.

Results and values ​​achieved

  • Lessons learned

    • Prompt quality is a first-class engineering lever, not a ‘magic words’ lottery - and it directly determines both output quality and dollar cost, because tokens are billable.

    • A great prompt is composed of seven components (Role, Instruction, Context, Input, Output Format, Examples, Constraints). Writing prompts that omit 4-5 of these is the usual reason results feel generic.

    • The seven principles (be clear, use directive language, use delimiters, describe DOs not DON’Ts, don’t ask LLMs to do math, allow ‘I don’t know’, break long inputs) are cheap, immediate quality wins - and they apply to every daily prompt, not only to ‘production’ ones.

    • Advanced techniques (CoT, ToT, Self-Consistency, RAG, Role Prompting) are variations on a single insight: structure the model’s ’thinking space’ so it can do better work, not more work.

    • Token economics are now a serious concern at student scale too. At Opus 4.6 prices, a verbose prompt is no longer free; this shapes how I budget prompts in group projects.

    • AWS Bedrock + Lambda + API Gateway + Cognito + S3 + CloudFront + DynamoDB + CloudWatch is a complete, repeatable template for shipping a small LLM product end-to-end, with no EC2 to manage. This is now the architecture pattern I will copy in my own capstones.

  • Newly Acquired Skills

    • Prompt Anatomy Skill: the ability to look at any prompt and quickly check which of the seven components are missing (Role, Instruction, Context, Input, Output Format, Examples, Constraints) and rewrite accordingly. Already applied to my study-personal-tutor prompts.

    • Principle-Based Prompt Review: a checklist of the seven principles (be clear, directive, delimit, DOs not DON’Ts, no math, allow uncertainty, segment). I now self-review every prompt before sending it to a model, which surfaces avoidable problems in 30 seconds.

    • AWS Bedrock + Serverless LLM Architecture: clear mental model of how a small LLM product is shipped on AWS - Bedrock for the model, Lambda for logic, API Gateway for routing, Cognito for identity, S3 + CloudFront for the frontend, DynamoDB for state, CloudWatch for observability. A blueprint I can copy verbatim into my own capstone.

    • Token-Cost Awareness: a working sense of frontier-model pricing (Opus 4.6: $5 / 1M input, $25 / 1M output). Lets me reason about prompts as budget line items rather than free form text.

    • Soft Skills: sharper listening during an engineering-heavy talk that mixed theory, pricing slides and architecture diagrams; structured note-taking that separated prompt principles, advanced techniques and architecture; clearer questions about pricing, latency and how to ship on AWS Bedrock with a small student budget.

Closing Note

Nguyen Tuan Thinh’s talk did something useful - it took the ‘art’ label off prompt engineering and replaced it with a craft that any student can practise today. The seven components and seven principles gave me a checklist I can apply to every prompt I write tomorrow morning, the advanced techniques (CoT, ToT, Self-Consistency, RAG, Role) lifted my ceiling on what one prompt can actually achieve, and the Proptimizer architecture showed that a real LLM product can be shipped student-quietly on AWS without touching EC2. As a student I walked away with cheaper, more reliable day-to-day prompts, a much clearer mental model of serverless LLM systems on AWS, and a concrete, template-ready plan for what my next class project should look like.

3. AI-Ready Fresher - Skills and Mindsets in the AI Era

A short, sharp career-and-mindset talk aimed at university students and recent graduates who are about to enter the AI-shaped job market. The session reframed AI as a multiplier, not a replacement, walked through the human foundations (growth mindset, integrity, the three circles of career fulfilment, the five benefits of work that go beyond salary, the five dimensions a company uses to evaluate you), and closed with a concrete next-actions list (ask why, build a long-term roadmap, form a team). Tone was mentor-style, with very practical lessons rather than AI hype.

Speakers

  • Khang – Solution Architect at Cloud Kinetics

Summary of content and main activities

Khang opened with an honest observation prompt (‘How has AI changed you lately?’) and anchored the talk in one of its sharpest lines: ‘You can outsource your thinking but you cannot outsource your understanding.’ He then framed AI as an amplifier - AI can empower you or demote you, and the stronger your foundation, the more AI can do for you. From there he moved into the human foundations (growth mindset via ‘why?’, integrity, the three circles of passion/benefit/responsibility, the five benefits of work beyond salary, the five dimensions a company uses to evaluate a fresher) and closed with three next actions: keep asking why, form a long-term roadmap that ’looks far, starts small’, and intentionally form a team, summarising with the Vietnamese proverb ‘di nhanh di mot minh, di xa di cung team’ (go fast alone, go far together).

  • Activity 1 — Opening Prompt: ‘How Has AI Changed You Lately?’

    • Began with a reflective question rather than a slide of stats. Invited each attendee to notice how AI has already altered their study, work and decision-making. Made the audience the protagonist of the talk instead of the speaker.

  • Activity 2 - The Headline Quote: ‘Outsource Thinking, Not Understanding’

    • Anchored the talk with a single sharp line: ‘You can outsource your thinking but you cannot outsource your understanding.’ Set the rule of the next 30 minutes: AI can do the typing for you, but the structure in your head is still yours to build.

  • Activity 3 — AI as Amplifier - Empower or Demote

    • Walked both sides of ‘Does AI make you lazy?’. Yes, it can, and students who only ever ask AI for answers finish the month with weaker fundamentals. No, it can also empower you by freeing time for deeper work. The answer depends entirely on the strength of your foundation.

  • Activity 4 — The Foundation Rule: Stronger Foundation, More AI Can Do

    • Drew the formula explicitly: the stronger your fundamentals, the more AI can do for you. This is not a slogan - it is the rule that turns AI from a crutch into a multiplier.

  • Activity 5 — Closing the Observation: ‘AI Is a Multiplier, Not a Replacement’

    • Stated the closing thesis of section I. ‘AI is a multiplier, not a replacement. Invest in your fundamentals - and let AI amplify your potential.’ Encouraged the audience to treat fundamentals as the actual competitive moat in this era.

  • Activity 6 — ‘Why?’ Section Opener - Growth Mindset

    • Transitioned from observation to the value foundations the rest of the talk would rebuild on. Picked growth mindset as the first foundation because it determines whether AI accelerates you or replaces you in five years.

  • Activity 7 - Growth Mindset Habit 1 - Question Everything (Ask ‘Why?’)

    • First habit. Always ask ‘why?’ - curiosity is the greatest asset a fresher has. Without it, AI will simply feed back generic answers and you will accept them.

  • Activity 8 - Growth Mindset Habit 2 - Embrace Mistakes

    • Second habit. At the early-career stage, mistakes are lessons, not failures. AI makes this even more important because you can iterate safely and cheaply, so you have no excuse not to.

  • Activity 9 - Growth Mindset Habit 3 - Stay Hungry

    • Third habit. The moment you stop learning is the moment you fall behind. Connected directly back to the AI multiplier framing - if you stop, the multiplier silently turns against you.

  • Activity 10 - Growth Mindset Challenge - What Should You Learn First?

    • Acknowledged the real anxiety underneath. There are too many things to learn (cloud, AI, ML, system design, languages), so what should a fresher actually pick? Set up the ’three circles’ and ‘five dimensions’ frameworks as the answer.

  • Activity 11 - Integrity - Do Your Best Even When No One Is Watching

    • First non-technical foundation. Defined integrity as doing your best when no one is watching. Pointed out that in the AI era, shortcuts are easier than ever (paste a homework prompt, ship a copy-pasted solution), so visible character is what still differentiates a hire-ready fresher from a copy-paster.

  • Activity 12 - The Three Circles of Career Fulfilment

    • Introduced a classic career-strategy model. Circle 1: What you LOVE to do (Passion). Circle 2: What BENEFITS you (Benefits). Circle 3: What you HAVE to do (Responsibility). Career satisfaction lives in the overlap of all three circles - the single most useful mental diagram of the talk for freshers choosing between offers.

  • Activity 13 - The 5 Benefits of Work (Beyond Salary)

    • Reframed why we work. Salary alone is benefit number 1 - and not the most important one. (1) Salary. (2) Experience. (3) Network. (4) Knowledge. (5) Growth. Encouraged each student to weight offers by all five, not only by the offer letter number.

  • Activity 14 - The 5 Dimensions a Company Uses to Evaluate You

    • Switched from candidate-perspective to recruiter-perspective. Companies evaluate freshers along five dimensions. (1) Attitude (Thai do). (2) Competence (Trinh do). (3) Experience (Kinh nghiem). (4) Exposure (Trai nghiem). (5) Potential (To chat). Helped the audience stop over-indexing on #2 and under-indexing on #1 and #5.

  • Activity 15 - The AI-Era Implication of the 5 Dimensions

    • Connected the model back to AI. AI is now a baseline on Competence - everyone has access to the same answers. What a company cannot get from AI is your Attitude, your Exposure, and your Potential. Hence the talk’s deeper argument: invest in the human dimensions, not the AI-augmentable one.

  • Activity 16 - Next Action 1 - Continuous Inquiry (Always Ask ‘Why?’)

    • First concrete next action. Asking ‘why?’ is the cheapest habit a fresher can build. When the answer is unknown, learn to make an assumption to test - this is how you build the ‘understanding’ the headline quote says cannot be outsourced.

  • Activity 17 - Next Action 2 - Form a Long-Term Roadmap (‘Look Far, Start Small’)

    • Second next action. A long-term roadmap is essential but must obey the ’look far, start small’ principle. Pick a 3-5 year vision (e.g. AI-era solutions architect), then reverse-engineer the next 6-month steps. Avoid the trap of 100% short-term planning.

  • Activity 18 - Next Action 3 - Form a Team (Go Fast Alone, Go Far Together)

    • Third next action. Invite explicitly to form or join a team rather than go it alone. Closed with the Vietnamese proverb: ‘Di nhanh di mot minh, di xa di cung team’ - go fast, go alone; go far, go together. Especially relevant for AI-era projects where no single student has all the skills.

  • Activity 19 - Q&A and Personal Reflections

    • Open floor where students asked about how to recover a weak foundation, how to switch from non-CS backgrounds, and whether to chase ‘AI hype’ skills or fundamentals first. Speaker’s standing answer was the same in every case: nail the fundamentals first, then layer AI on top.

  • Activity 20 - Closing: Invest in Fundamentals, Let AI Amplify

    • Closing summary. Pulled every section back to the same line: ‘AI is a multiplier, not a replacement. Invest in your fundamentals - and let AI amplify your potential.’ Exit slide invited students to keep asking ‘why?’ and to walk out with one next action they would execute this week.

Results and values ​​achieved

  • Lessons learned

    • AI is a multiplier, not a replacement - and the same person can both benefit and be demoted by AI depending solely on the strength of their fundamentals.

    • ‘You can outsource your thinking, but you cannot outsource your understanding’ is a rule worth tattooing on a fresher’s notebook - it is the single line that separates AI users from AI-augmented engineers.

    • Growth mindset is not a buzzword in the AI era - it is the operating system that decides whether the multiplier goes up or down. Curiosity + embracing mistakes + staying hungry are the three inputs that feed the OS.

    • Salary is benefit number 1 of 5, not benefit number 1 ranking. The other four - Experience, Network, Knowledge, Growth - usually matter more for a fresher’s first few years. Picking the highest-paid offer can easily be the wrong choice.

    • Companies evaluate freshers on Attitude + Potential + Exposure as much as on Competence. AI has now collapsed Competence to a baseline, so the human dimensions are exactly where a fresher can differentiate.

    • A long-term roadmap that obeys ’look far, start small’ is the practical antidote to AI-era overwhelm. The team you form along the way is what carries you through the years between ‘far’ and ‘small’.

  • Newly Acquired Skills

    • AI-Era Mental Model: a clear, non-hype picture of how AI interacts with a junior career - multiplier rule, foundation dependency and the three circles/5 dimensions/5 benefits lens to read any next opportunity (internship, certification, project).

    • Always-Ask-Why Discipline: a behavioural habit that turns ‘copy an AI answer’ into ‘understand the answer before shipping it’. Already applied to my weekly study prompts and to PR comments in the group project.

    • Three-Circles Decision Lens: practical ability to evaluate a job, project or community activity against the three circles (Passion / Benefits / Responsibility), which prevents pure-money or pure-hype decisions early in a career.

    • Five-Benefits Offer Reading: the habit of scoring any new opportunity on all five dimensions (Salary, Experience, Network, Knowledge, Growth) rather than only on the offer letter, immediately useful for choosing between internships and scholarships.

    • Look-Far-Start-Small Roadmap: a concrete way to write a 3-5 year vision and reverse-engineer the next 6-month steps, linked to a team. Helps me stop chasing every new shiny tool and instead pick a small set to deeply invest in.

    • Soft Skills: sharper listening during a career-and-mindset talk, structured note-taking that separates ‘quote’ from ‘framework’ from ’next action’, and clearer questions about what to do when one’s foundation feels weak (the correct answer: smaller projects, lower stakes, ship anyway, ship more often).

Closing Note

Khang’s session was a calm, mentor-style reminder that the AI era does not require a different student - it requires a more intentional one. The talk collapsed the loud ‘will AI take my job?’ anxiety into a quiet, sharper question: is your foundation strong enough for AI to amplify you, and are you building your next move around the human dimensions (attitude, exposure, potential) that AI cannot replace? By the end of the thirty minutes I had a cleaner mental model of how to read offers, projects and even my own week against something better than the salary line, and a sentence I keep repeating to myself since: ‘Invest in your fundamentals - and let AI amplify your potential.’

4. BMAD Method - The New Era of Context Engineering

A hands-on framework introduction talk aimed at developers, students and AI builders who are already tired of ‘Vibe Coding’ producing demo-grade, unmaintainable software. The session explained why one-prompt-fits-all AI coding breaks at production scale (Context Bloat, no SDLC, hallucination), then walked the audience through BMAD - Breakthrough Method for Agile AI Driven Development - as a structured ‘Context Engineering’ framework that puts a full Agile-and-AI team (Orchestrator, PM, Architect, PO, Scrum Master, Dev, Reviewer) inside the developer IDE. The talk covered the three-phase pipeline (Planning -> Sharding/Scrum -> Execution), Context Isolation as a hallucination countermeasure, Scale-Domain-Adaptive agents, Party Mode for cross-agent debate, the BMM ecosystem (CIS, BMB, TEA, BMGD), and the actual install command for Cursor, Windsurf and Claude Code.

Speakers

  • Team VIB – the creators and presenters of the UTMorpho project.

Summary of content and main activities

The speaker opened by stating that ‘Vibe Coding is only for demos, not for production-ready software’ - one prompt (‘Build me an app’) handed to a single chat ignores the Software Development Life Cycle, leads to Context Bloat, produces patchy code and cannot scale. He then contrasted the old ‘prompting’ mindset with the new ‘context engineering’ mindset across four dimensions: orientation (one vs. many tasks), documentation (none vs. PRD plus architecture), context (one giant blob vs. isolated context per agent) and complexity handling (breaks vs. Scale-Domain-Adaptive). From there he decoded BMAD as a ‘Breakthrough Method for Agile AI Driven Development’, introduced the agent dream team (Orchestrator, PM, Architect, PO, Scrum Master, Dev and Reviewer), walked through the three-phase pipeline (Ideation -> PRD -> Architecture -> Sharding -> Epics -> Stories -> Code -> Review -> Done), explained Context Isolation as the hallucination countermeasure, and finished with Party Mode, the BMM ecosystem, system requirements and the install command.

  • Activity 1 — Framing: The New Era of Context Engineering

    • Set the era framing. We are leaving ‘prompting’ behind and entering ‘context engineering’ - a discipline in which the question is no longer what to type into the chat but what context, persona and workflow to give the model.

  • Activity 2 - Lead Thesis: Vibe Coding Is for Demos, Not for Production

    • Stated the core thesis up front. Single-prompt vibe coding produces demo-grade software and breaks the moment it needs to be extended, tested or shipped to real users.

  • Activity 3 - Anatomy of the Vibe Coding Failure

    • Walked the failure mode explicitly. Expectation: a single command such as ‘Create an app for me’ where one chat handles design, code and quality. Reality: the AI skips the Software Development Life Cycle, the context bloats, code becomes patchy, the model loses direction and the project stops being extendable.

  • Activity 4 - The Shift: From Free Prompting to Context Engineering

    • Introduced the four-axis comparison between old vibe coding and the new BMAD method. (1) Orientation: one prompt that does everything vs. Agile-standard task breakdown. (2) Documentation: blind code with no prior design vs. clear PRD and System Architecture. (3) Context: a single over-stuffed file vs. strictly separated contexts for each agent. (4) Complexity: breaks under scale vs. Scale-Domain-Adaptive (self-adjusting analysis depth).

  • Activity 5 — Decoding BMAD: Breakthrough Method for Agile AI Driven Development

    • Made the acronym explicit. BMAD stands for Breakthrough Method for Agile AI Driven Development. It is a methodology, not a model - and the breakthrough comes from putting the entire Agile ritual (analyst, PM, architect, PO, Scrum Master, dev, reviewer) into the AI loop.

  • Activity 6 — The Dream Team of Agents

    • Introduced the six core personas. Orchestrator (leads the global process). PM / Product Manager (writes the PRD). Architect (designs the system and picks the tech stack). PO / Product Owner (shards the documents). Scrum Master (organises Epics and Stories). Dev and Reviewer (writes code and runs a closed-loop review). Each persona has its own context and its own discipline.

  • Activity 7 - Principle 1 - Scale-Domain-Adaptive Agents

    • First key principle. The agents automatically adjust their analysis depth to the size of the task. A small bug fix gets a small, fast response; an enterprise system gets a deep, multi-document response. No more one-size-fits-all AI output.

  • Activity 8 - Principle 2 - Closed-Loop Pipeline Across Three Phases

    • Second key principle. The work moves through three phases: Planning (idea -> PRD -> Architecture), Sharding (cut documents into Epics and Stories, then Draft -> Approved), and Execution (one Story at a time -> code -> review -> done). Each phase has explicit deliverables, not vibes.

  • Activity 9 - Principle 3 - Context Isolation Against Hallucination

    • Third key principle. Each agent starts in a brand-new chat session, with a strict context split. The orchestrator hands each agent only the slice of context it needs, which dramatically lowers LLM hallucination. Counter-intuitively, less context can mean smarter answers.

  • Activity 10 - Phase 1 Deep-Dive - Planning: Idea -> PRD -> Architecture

    • Walked through phase 1. The Orchestrator collects the idea. The PM persona turns it into a Product Requirements Document. The Architect persona turns the PRD into a System Architecture document and tech-stack proposal. Output of this phase is two human-reviewable artefacts, not committed code.

  • Activity 11 - Phase 2 Deep-Dive - Sharding and Scrum: Epics and Stories

    • Walked through phase 2. The PO persona shards the PRD and the Architecture into smaller documents. The Scrum Master persona turns the sharded documents into Epics and Stories in a tree structure. Each Story moves through two states: Draft and Approved. Only Approved Stories pass into phase 3. This is the exact same gating as a real Agile team.

  • Activity 12 - Phase 3 Deep-Dive - Execution: Code -> Review -> Done

    • Walked through phase 3. The Dev persona picks exactly one Approved Story at a time, breaks it into sub-tasks, writes code and hands it to the Reviewer persona. The Reviewer runs a closed loop: Refactor -> Fix -> Done. The Story is shipped only when the loop closes cleanly. No commit-and-pray.

  • Activity 13 - Comparing BMAD vs Other AI Coding Approaches

    • Stacked BMAD against alternative approaches. Compared with single-prompt vibe coding, BMAD adds SDLC and personas. Compared with naive agent loops, BMAD adds gated Draft -> Approved story states and explicit context isolation. Compared with rigid traditional process, BMAD is light enough for a student team to install with one npm command.

  • Activity 14 - Party Mode - Multi-Agent Discussion in One Chat

    • Introduced Party Mode. For complex decisions, multiple personas (for example Dev and Architect) can be brought into a single, model-mediated discussion. Each persona defends its point of view before the team converges. This is BMAD equivalent of a design review meeting.

  • Activity 15 - The BMM Ecosystem (CIS, BMB, TEA, BMGD)

    • Introduced the broader BMAD Method ecosystem. CIS (Creative Inspiration Suite) for non-software creative work. BMB (BMAD Builder) for creating new custom agents. TEA (Test and Evaluation Architect) for testing depth. BMGD (BMAD Game Development Studio) for game development. Plug-ins, not forks.

  • Activity 16 - Practical Workflow Example (Idea to Production)

    • Walked the audience through a concrete example: an idea for a small B2B SaaS becomes a PRD via the PM agent, turns into a hub-and-spoke serverless architecture via the Architect agent, gets sharded into 12 Epics and 47 Stories, and finishes 8 weeks later with each Story having gone through the Review loop. Made the framework tangible.

  • Activity 17 - System Requirements for BMAD

    • Listed the hard requirements. Node.js v20 or newer. Python 3.10 or newer. A modern package manager (uv for Python). An IDE on the supported list. Each agent rules and prompts are stored inside the project, keeping data local and secure.

  • Activity 18 - IDE Support and Installation

    • Showed the install path. Supported IDEs: Cursor, Windsurf and Claude Code. Install command: npx bmad-method@next install . (run from the project root). The setup is per-project, so each student team can install it independently and keep their rules isolated.

  • Activity 19 - Why BMAD Fits Student Capstone Scale

    • Argued that BMAD is unusually friendly to small teams. A 3-4 person student group can act like a 9-person Agile company: PM agent writes the PRD, Architect agent designs the stack, Scrum Master agent tracks Stories, Dev agent codes one Approved Story at a time. Quality discipline without hiring discipline.

  • Activity 20 - Q&A and Adoption Stories

    • Open floor where developers asked about handling LM token costs, integrating BMAD with existing GitHub Actions, and whether to switch mid-project. The recurring advice was the same: pilot on a new small repo, don’t try to retrofit a stale codebase.

Results and values ​​achieved

  • Lessons learned

    • Vibe coding is not AI development - it is AI demo. The moment a project needs to be extended, tested or shipped, the context-bloated single chat stops being able to keep up.

    • Context engineering is the actual discipline for AI-era production software - not a better prompt, but who gets which slice of context, at which step, with which role.

    • A small team plus multiple specialised AI agents can deliver with the discipline of a much larger Agile company - the size of the team is now decoupled from the rigour of the process.

    • Isolated context per agent is a hallucination countermeasure more powerful than better prompts - giving the model less and asking it to do one thing at a time is what actually works.

    • Gating Stories through a Draft -> Approved state is just as valuable with AI agents as with humans. Without that gate, every AI commit becomes uncorrectable debt.

    • BMAD real value is not the agents themselves - it is the closed-loop pipeline (Idea -> PRD -> Architecture -> Epics -> Stories -> Code -> Review -> Done) that turns AI experimentation into a repeatable process.

  • Newly Acquired Skills

    • Context Engineering Mental Model: the ability to design any AI-assisted coding workflow as a question of who owns which context, persona and step rather than what prompt should I type - reusable beyond BMAD itself.

    • Scale-Domain-Adaptive Sizing: the practical habit of asking the AI to size its output to the actual problem (a 10-line fix vs. an enterprise system) instead of forcing one mode on every interaction. Useful for writing PRs, designing demos and grading classmate code.

    • Story-Level Gating Discipline: the ability to apply the Draft -> Approved gate to AI-generated changes, just as a Scrum Master would gate human stories. Strong defense against AI-generated sprawl sneaking into the codebase.

    • Context-Split Hygiene: a working habit of starting fresh chat sessions for distinct sub-tasks (architecture vs. schema vs. test data) instead of stacking everything into a single growing context. Already visible quality improvement on my own side projects.

    • Party Mode Facilitation: the meta-skill of asking multiple AI personas for distinct viewpoints and reconciling them (e.g. would the architect agent and the dev agent agree on this trade-off?) - useful even when not running BMAD itself.

    • Soft Skills: sharper listening during a process-heavy framework talk; structured note-taking that separates principles, agents and pipeline; clearer questions about how to install and adopt BMAD inside a small student team without bogging us down in process.

Closing Note

This session quietly re-classified what doing AI development actually means. The argument was not AI is overhyped do it less, but AI is overhyped at the level of a single prompt - do it better with a process. By the end of the talk I had a recognisable alternative to Vibe Coding that I can install in the IDE with one command, a vocabulary (PRD, Architecture, Epics, Stories, Draft, Approved) that already improves our group project, and a rule of thumb I will carry into every future AI-assisted coding session: smaller context per agent, gated story flow, and an explicit design before any code touches the keyboard.
